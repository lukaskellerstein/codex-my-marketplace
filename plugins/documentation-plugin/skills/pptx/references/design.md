# Design Reference

## Color Palettes

### Choosing a Palette

1. **Identify the audience**: Executives → muted/premium. Creatives → bold/saturated. Students → high-contrast/accessible.
2. **Match the energy**: Product launch → high energy. Quarterly review → low energy. Pitch deck → high energy with premium feel.
3. **Dark or light base?**: If presenting in person (projector), prefer light. If sharing digitally/on screens, dark themes look more premium.
4. **One dominant color**: The primary should cover 60-70% of visual space. The secondary fills 20-30%. Accent is 5-10% max.
5. **Never pick a palette just because it "looks nice"** — it should feel inevitable for THIS topic.

If using a design template from [templates.md](templates.md), use the template's recommended palette.

### Color Usage Rules

- **Dark/light sandwich**: Use dark backgrounds for title + conclusion slides, light for content slides. Or commit to dark throughout for a premium feel.
- **Dominance**: One color should dominate. Never give all colors equal weight.
- **Text on dark backgrounds**: Use `F8FAFC` or `E2E8F0` for body text — never pure `FFFFFF` on body text (too bright). Pure white OK for headlines.
- **Text on light backgrounds**: Use the primary color or a dark neutral (`333333`, `1A1A1A`).
- **Accent color**: Reserve for call-to-action elements, key numbers, icons, or small highlights. Never use as a background.

## Dark Theme Design System

Dark presentations feel premium and modern. They work especially well for digital-first presentations (shared via email/Slack), tech/AI topics, pitch decks, and any topic where you want to feel "cutting edge."

### Dark Theme Rules

- **Background**: Use `0D0D0D`, `111111`, `1A1A1A`, or `0F172A` — never pure `000000` (too harsh).
- **Primary text**: `F8FAFC` or `E2E8F0` — never pure `FFFFFF` on body text (too bright). Pure white OK for headlines.
- **Secondary text**: `94A3B8` or `9CA3AF` — muted gray for captions and labels.
- **Cards on dark**: Use `1E293B` or `1F2937` fill with subtle shadow, NOT white cards on dark backgrounds (too jarring).
- **Accent colors**: Bright, saturated accents (teal `14B8A6`, violet `8B5CF6`, amber `F59E0B`) pop beautifully on dark.
- **Photo overlays**: Use 50-65% transparency on dark backgrounds for photo slides.
- **Avoid**: Low-contrast gray-on-gray. If you have to squint, it's too subtle.

## Typography Scale

| Element | Size | Weight | Notes |
|---------|------|--------|-------|
| Title slide headline | 44-60pt | Bold | Go bigger for single-word impact |
| Slide title | 28-36pt | Bold | Consistent across all content slides |
| Section header | 20-24pt | Bold | Within a slide, for sub-sections |
| Body text | 14-16pt | Regular | Left-aligned, not centered |
| Bullet items | 14-16pt | Regular | Same as body |
| Captions / footnotes | 10-12pt | Regular | Muted color (lighter gray) |
| Big stat callout | 64-96pt | Bold | Let the number BE the visual |
| Stat label (below number) | 12-14pt | Regular | Describes what the number means |
| Impact word | 72-96pt | Bold | Single-word slides for section dividers |

### Typography as Design Element

- **Hero numbers**: Stats displayed at 72-96pt feel more impactful than 48pt. Let the number BE the visual.
- **Dramatic size contrast**: Title at 48pt + body at 14pt creates visual hierarchy that 28pt + 14pt cannot.
- **One-word impact**: A slide with just "WHY?" at 80pt centered on a dark background is more memorable than a bullet list.
- **Character spacing on headers**: Use `charSpacing: 4-8` on uppercase headers for a premium, editorial feel.
- **Large pull quotes**: 28-36pt italic quotes with generous margins feel editorial, not generic.

### Typography Rules

- **Left-align body text and bullets** — center only titles and callout numbers.
- **Size contrast matters** — titles need 28pt+ to stand apart from 14pt body. If they look similar in size, the hierarchy is broken.
- **One font pair per deck** — never mix more than 2 font families.
- **Line height** — use `paraSpaceAfter` (not `lineSpacing`) for spacing between bullet items. Values of 6-10pt work well.

## Spacing

| Element | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Slide edge margins | 0.5" minimum on all sides |
| Between content blocks | 0.3-0.5" |
| Between title and content | 0.3-0.5" |
| Between bullet items | 6-10pt `paraSpaceAfter` |
| Card/shape internal padding | 0.2-0.3" |
| Between cards/columns | 0.3-0.4" |

### Spacing Rules

- **Leave breathing room** — don't fill every square inch. White space is a feature.
- **Consistent gaps** — pick either 0.3" or 0.5" as your standard gap and use it throughout.
- **Content zone** — the safe content area on a 16:9 slide is roughly x: 0.5-9.5, y: 0.5-5.125.
- **Footer zone** — if using a footer bar, it typically goes at y: 5.125 with height 0.5".
- **Dramatic whitespace** — large empty areas draw the eye to content. Don't fear using only 40% of a slide.

## Visual Motifs (Pick ONE Per Deck)

A motif is a single visual element that repeats across your deck to create cohesion. Pick ONE and commit:

1. **Rounded image frames**: All images clipped to rounded rectangles (0.12-0.15" radius). No other decorative shapes.
2. **Color-block headers**: A solid-color band at the top of every content slide. Title sits inside.
3. **Side accent strip**: A narrow (0.15") vertical strip in the accent color along the left edge of content slides.
4. **Corner badge**: A small rounded rect in one corner with a page number or section icon.
5. **Generous whitespace**: No decorative elements at all. Let spacing and typography do the work.

**NEVER combine multiple motifs.** If you have accent bars AND decorative circles AND colored headers, you have visual noise, not design.

**NEVER use thin horizontal lines under titles.** This is the #1 AI-generated slide signature.

**NEVER add the same decorative circle to every slide.** If an OVAL appears on every single slide at the same coordinates, it becomes noise.

## Premium Design Techniques

### Full-Bleed Photo Backgrounds with Dark Overlay

The single most impactful technique. Use a relevant stock photo (sourced from Unsplash via the `image-sourcing` skill) as a full-bleed background, then overlay a semi-transparent dark RECTANGLE so text remains readable.

```javascript
const photoData = fs.readFileSync("photo.jpg");
slide.background = { data: "image/jpeg;base64," + photoData.toString("base64") };

slide.addShape(pres.shapes.RECTANGLE, {
  x: 0, y: 0, w: 10, h: 5.625,
  fill: { color: "000000", transparency: 45 }
});
```

Adjust `transparency` (0-100) to control darkness. 40-50 for most photos. Darker photos need less overlay (50-60), lighter need more (30-40).

**When to use:** Title slides, section dividers, closing slides, quote slides. Aim for at least 2-3 photo-backed slides per deck.

### Creating Depth (The #1 Way to Look Professional)

#### Elevated Cards (Varied by Importance)

Don't use the same shadow on every card. Vary shadow intensity by importance:

- **Primary card** (most important): `shadow: { blur: 12, offset: 4, opacity: 0.15 }`
- **Secondary cards**: `shadow: { blur: 6, offset: 2, opacity: 0.08 }`
- **Subtle containers**: `shadow: { blur: 3, offset: 1, opacity: 0.05 }`

Always use factory functions — PptxGenJS mutates option objects. See [pptxgenjs-api.md](pptxgenjs-api.md).

#### Dark Elevated Cards

On dark backgrounds, use dark surface colors instead of white:

```javascript
const makeDarkCard = () => ({
  fill: { color: "1E293B" }, // dark surface
  rectRadius: 0.12,
  shadow: { type: "outer", color: "000000", blur: 10, offset: 3, angle: 135, opacity: 0.3 }
});
```

#### Overlapping Elements

Modern slides allow elements to overlap intentionally:
- An image that extends 0.5" behind a text card
- A stat number that overlaps the top edge of its container
- A photo that breaks the grid by extending to the edge while text stays in margins

#### Background Layering

Instead of flat solid backgrounds, layer subtle elements:
- Base color + one large, very faint (90-95% transparency) OVAL for texture
- Photo background + dark overlay + content = 3 layers of depth
- Don't add decorative shapes UNLESS they serve this layering purpose

### Gradient-Style Backgrounds

PptxGenJS doesn't support native gradient fills. Two approaches:

**Approach 1: Source or generate a gradient image** — use the `image-generation` skill to create a gradient background at 1920x1080, then set as slide background.

**Approach 2: Layered OVALs** — one large, faint OVAL creates a subtle color shift:

```javascript
slide.background = { color: "0F172A" };
slide.addShape(pres.shapes.OVAL, {
  x: 5, y: 2, w: 8, h: 6,
  fill: { color: "4A90D9", transparency: 85 }
});
```

### Color-Blocked Sections

Split the slide into color blocks using RECTANGLE shapes:

```javascript
// Dark top band for title
slide.addShape(pres.shapes.RECTANGLE, {
  x: 0, y: 0, w: 10, h: 1.2,
  fill: { color: primary }
});
slide.background = { color: "F5F5F5" };
```

## Deck Rhythm Principles

1. **Bookend with impact**: First and last slides should share visual DNA (same photo, same color, mirrored layout).
2. **Alternate visual weight**: After a dense content slide, follow with a breathing slide (photo, big quote, or single stat).
3. **No more than 2 consecutive slides with the same background treatment**: If you have 3 white slides in a row, inject a dark or photo slide between them.
4. **Section dividers reset the eye**: Use a dark/photo slide as a section divider to signal "new topic."
5. **One idea per slide**: If a slide has a title AND bullets AND a chart AND a photo, split it.
6. **End strong**: The closing slide should feel as polished as the title slide.

## Common Design Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Repeating the same layout** — vary between columns, cards, callouts, and charts.
2. **Centering body text** — left-align paragraphs and bullets. Center only titles.
3. **Weak size contrast** — if your title and body look similar in size, increase the title to 36-48pt.
4. **Defaulting to blue** — choose colors that reflect the specific topic.
5. **Inconsistent spacing** — pick a standard gap and stick with it.
6. **Text-only slides** — every slide should have a visual element. Source real photos from Unsplash first via `image-sourcing`; only AI-generate when no suitable stock photo is available.
7. **Too many bullet points** — if a slide has more than 5 bullets, split it.
8. **Low-contrast text** — test that text is legible against its background.
9. **Accent lines under titles** — hallmark of AI-generated slides. Use white space or background color changes instead.
10. **All slides with white backgrounds** — use the dark/light sandwich or commit to dark throughout.
11. **Same decorative shapes on every slide** — this is noise, not design. Pick ONE motif.
12. **No photography** — source topic-relevant photos for at least title, section divider, and closing slides.
13. **Generic imagery** — "Abstract blue wave" is lazy. "Aerial view of solar farm at sunset" is specific and impactful.
14. **Georgia + Calibri on every deck** — pick fonts that match the topic's personality. See [Font Pairings](#font-pairings).
15. **Small stat numbers** — stats at 48pt look timid. Use 64-96pt and let the number dominate.
16. **Evenly-spaced symmetrical layouts** — modern design uses asymmetry and dramatic whitespace.
