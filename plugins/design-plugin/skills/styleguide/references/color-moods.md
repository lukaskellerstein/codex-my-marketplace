# Color Moods Reference

These are strategic color directions organized by emotional intent and industry context. They provide starting points for brand identity and UI theming decisions, not finished palettes.

For generating production-ready palettes with precise token values, and for running WCAG contrast checks, use **design-system**.

| Accent | #3182CE | Royal Blue |

### Cultural Associations

- **Western markets:** Blue signals reliability, professionalism, and security. It is the most universally "safe" brand color.
- **East Asian markets:** Blue can carry connotations of immortality and advancement. Dark blue is associated with trust in banking.
- **Middle Eastern markets:** Blue is protective and spiritual. It carries positive associations across most contexts.
- **Global caution:** Avoid pairing blue with black in contexts where mourning associations are undesirable.

### Industries

Finance, healthcare, enterprise software, consulting, insurance, legal services, government

### Common Mistakes

- Using the exact same shade of navy as every competitor, resulting in zero brand differentiation.
- Choosing a blue so muted it reads as gray on screens, losing the calming psychological effect entirely.
- Over-relying on blue and neglecting a strong accent color, which makes CTAs invisible.
- Pairing blue with a warm gray that clashes instead of a cool neutral that harmonizes.

### Dark Mode Adaptation

- Shift primary blues from dark-on-light to medium-bright on dark backgrounds (e.g., #1E3A5F becomes #5B9BD5 as a text/icon color on dark surfaces).
- Keep accent colors warm to maintain visual hierarchy contrast against cooled-down blues.
- Use slate-900 (#0F172A) or blue-gray-900 (#1A202C) as background, not pure black.
- Reduce saturation slightly on secondary blues to prevent eye strain on dark surfaces.

### Cultural Associations

- **Western markets:** Red and orange convey urgency, excitement, appetite, and action. Used heavily in clearance and sale contexts.
- **East Asian markets:** Red is auspicious, celebratory, and prosperous. Orange signals happiness and good health.
- **South Asian markets:** Orange/saffron carries spiritual and cultural significance. Use respectfully.
- **Global caution:** Red can mean danger, debt, or error. Context matters enormously when using red as a primary color.

### Industries

Marketing agencies, entertainment, food and beverage, sports, events, gaming, retail promotions

### Common Mistakes

- Using saturated red as the dominant surface color, which causes visual fatigue within seconds.
- Neglecting accessibility. Bright orange and red on white often fail WCAG AA contrast ratios.
- Deploying high-energy palettes for content-heavy interfaces where reading comfort matters.
- Forgetting that energy colors need generous breathing room (whitespace) to avoid overwhelming the user.

### Dark Mode Adaptation

- Desaturate reds and corals by 10-15% to prevent them from appearing to "vibrate" on dark backgrounds.
- Shift background to a warm dark tone (#1A1A1A or #2D1B1B) rather than cold black, which clashes with warm palettes.
- Use the neutral as a subtle warm tint in dark card surfaces to maintain the energetic feel.
- Ensure red-on-dark passes WCAG AA. Often #FF6B6B works, but #E74C3C may need lightening.

### Cultural Associations

- **Western markets:** Greens signal nature, health, growth, and environmental responsibility. Teal communicates sophistication within the wellness space.
- **East Asian markets:** Green represents fertility, youth, and eternity. Jade green has particular cultural prestige.
- **Middle Eastern markets:** Green is the color of Islam and carries deep respect. Use intentionally and appropriately.
- **Global caution:** Muted greens can read as institutional or clinical if not balanced with warmth.

### Industries

### Common Mistakes

- Making the palette so muted that it becomes monotonous with no clear visual hierarchy.
- Using too many greens without a contrasting accent, which makes CTAs disappear into the page.
- Choosing earth tones that look muddy on lower-quality screens.
- Confusing "calm" with "boring." Even wellness brands need moments of contrast and visual interest.

### Dark Mode Adaptation

- Use deep forest tones (#1A2F23) or dark teal (#0D2B2B) as background instead of black.
- Lighten sage and green primaries significantly for text/icon use on dark surfaces (#81B29A or similar).
- Earth-tone accents (clays, sands) translate well to dark mode with minimal adjustment.
- Preserve warmth in neutrals: use dark warm grays (#2D2B28) for card surfaces.

### Cultural Associations

- **Western markets:** Purple and electric blue signal cutting-edge technology, creativity, and futurism. Neon accents suggest speed and digital-native identity.
- **East Asian markets:** Purple carries associations with nobility and spirituality. Tech brands in Japan and Korea lean into neon palettes heavily.
- **Global tech culture:** Dark backgrounds with neon accents have become a universal visual language for "developer-facing" and "advanced technology."
- **Global caution:** Purple can read as spiritual or mystical in some contexts, which may undermine a serious enterprise positioning.

### Industries

AI/ML products, developer tools, startups, crypto and blockchain, space technology, cybersecurity, data visualization

### Common Mistakes

- **The purple gradient cliche.** A purple-to-blue gradient is so overused in tech branding that it has become visual wallpaper. If you choose this direction, you need a distinctive twist: an unexpected accent color, a unique application, or an unusual tonal range. Otherwise your brand will be indistinguishable from every other AI startup launched since 2020.
- Overusing neon accents. They should highlight, not dominate. A page full of neon is unreadable.
- Choosing dark backgrounds as a default without testing readability for long-form content.
- Relying solely on color to communicate "innovative." Color sets a tone, but without strong typography and layout it falls flat.

### Dark Mode Adaptation

- This mood category is often dark-first by nature. Ensure your light mode is equally considered.
- Neon accents (#00F5D4, #EC4899) work beautifully on dark backgrounds but need significant desaturation for light mode.
- Use layered dark surfaces (background at #0F0E17, cards at #1A1940, elevated elements at #252250) for depth.
- Test violet primaries on dark backgrounds carefully. Medium violets can appear washed out; either go lighter or darker, not in between.

| Secondary | #B8A9C9 | Lavender Silver |

| Neutral | #F8F5F0 | Warm Linen |

### Cultural Associations

- **Western markets:** Black and gold are the universal shorthand for premium. Minimalism (lots of negative space, restrained type) reinforces the luxury signal.
- **East Asian markets:** Gold and red are auspicious and luxurious. Deep jewel tones carry prestige.
- **Middle Eastern markets:** Gold, deep purple, and rich patterns are associated with opulence and hospitality.
- **Global caution:** Luxury palettes rely heavily on execution. Cheap typography or crowded layouts instantly undermine an expensive color story.

### Industries

Fashion and haute couture, jewelry, luxury real estate, premium automotive, high-end hospitality, fine dining, prestige cosmetics

### Common Mistakes

- Using gold as a flat hex color (#FFD700) instead of simulating metallic sheen through gradients and contextual contrast.
- Making everything dark and moody at the expense of usability. Even luxury brands need clear navigation and readable text.
- Overusing black. Luxury is about restraint and contrast, not about painting everything one color.
- Choosing a berry or plum secondary and then never using it, resulting in a palette that is effectively just black and gold.

### Dark Mode Adaptation

- Dark IS often the primary mode for luxury brands. Design dark-first and treat light mode as the adaptation.
- Gold accents on dark backgrounds are inherently high-impact. Ensure gold text passes contrast requirements (use #C9A84C or lighter on #1A1A1A).
- For light mode, invert carefully: use warm off-whites and let dark tones become the accent rather than the surface.
- Avoid pure white backgrounds for luxury light modes. Champagne (#F5F0EB), linen (#F8F5F0), or ivory (#FAF7F2) maintain the premium feel.

### Cultural Associations

- **Western markets:** Bright, saturated colors signal youth, fun, approachability, and informality. Pastels indicate gentleness and creativity.
- **East Asian markets:** Kawaii culture embraces pastels and rounded, friendly palettes. Bright colors are common in youth-oriented tech.

### Industries

### Common Mistakes

- Using every bright color at once. Even youthful palettes need a dominant color and supporting hierarchy.
- Ignoring accessibility. Bright yellow on white, or light pink on white, are WCAG failures. Pair vivid colors with dark text.
- Assuming youthful means informal in every element. The palette can be playful while the interface structure stays clean and organized.
- Choosing colors that look great in mockups but cause eye fatigue in daily use. Test with realistic content density and session length.

### Dark Mode Adaptation

- Bright and pastel colors need significant adjustment for dark mode. Reduce brightness by 10-20% and slightly increase saturation.
- Use dark charcoal (#1F2937) or dark blue-gray (#1E293B) as background, never pure black, to keep the friendly tone.
- Neon-ified versions of the core palette often work well: Bubblegum becomes a lighter, glowing pink on dark surfaces.
- Test carefully. Youthful palettes are the hardest category to adapt to dark mode while preserving the intended mood.

## Combining Moods

Single-mood palettes work for straightforward brand identities, but many products sit at the intersection of two moods. The key is choosing a dominant mood (70% of color usage) and a supporting mood (30%).

### Proven Combinations

**Trust + Innovation** (fintech, healthtech, enterprise AI)
- Lead with Trust blues for primary surfaces and navigation. Introduce Innovation violets or neon accents for feature highlights and data visualization.
- Example pairing: Navy (#1E3A5F) primary with Neon Mint (#00F5D4) accent.
- Trap to avoid: The result can feel cold and sterile. Add one warm neutral to humanize it.

**Calm + Luxury** (premium wellness, boutique hospitality)
- Lead with Calm earth tones and greens for the overall atmosphere. Use Luxury gold or deep berry for CTAs and premium tier indicators.
- Example pairing: Sage (#6B9080) primary with Antique Gold (#C9A84C) accent.
- Trap to avoid: The palette can feel dated or "hotel lobby." Keep typography modern and layout minimal.

**Energy + Fresh** (food delivery, fitness, youth entertainment)
- Lead with Energy corals or oranges for urgency and appetite appeal. Use Fresh limes or sky blues for supporting illustration and backgrounds.
- Example pairing: Living Coral (#FF6B6B) primary with Sky Cyan (#67E8F9) secondary.
- Trap to avoid: Visual chaos. This combination is loud by nature; enforce strict hierarchy and generous whitespace.

**Innovation + Luxury** (premium tech, high-end automotive, prestige gadgets)
- Lead with Luxury darks and golds for the brand shell. Use Innovation electric blues or neon accents for interactive elements and product showcases.
- Example pairing: Rich Black (#0D0D0D) primary with Electron Blue (#0984E3) accent and Muted Gold (#C9A84C) secondary.
- Trap to avoid: Looking like a generic "dark mode tech" product. The gold differentiator must be used consistently to maintain the luxury signal.

**Trust + Calm** (healthcare, therapy platforms, insurance)
- Lead with Trust blues for structure and wayfinding. Use Calm greens and earth tones for content areas and illustrations.
- Example pairing: Prussian Blue (#0F4C75) primary with Sage (#6B9080) secondary.
- Trap to avoid: The palette becomes visually monotone. Introduce a warm accent (clay, amber) to create focal points.

**Fresh + Innovation** (creative tools, social platforms, casual AI products)
- Lead with Fresh pastels or brights for the overall tone. Use Innovation violets and electric accents for interactive and "smart" features.
- Example pairing: Soft Violet (#A78BFA) primary with Electric Cyan (#22D3EE) accent.
- Trap to avoid: Losing the innovation signal in playfulness. Reserve the electric accent for moments tied to AI or smart features specifically.

### General Principles for Mood Mixing

1. **Pick a clear dominant.** If both moods fight for attention, the result reads as confused, not sophisticated.
2. **Map moods to interface layers.** One mood for the structural shell (navigation, backgrounds), another for interactive and highlight elements.
3. **Test the combination against competitor palettes.** The goal of mixing is differentiation. If the result looks like everyone else in the space, adjust the ratio or pick a different supporting mood.
4. **Validate with users.** Mood perception is subjective. Run quick preference tests with 5-10 target users before committing to a mixed palette.
